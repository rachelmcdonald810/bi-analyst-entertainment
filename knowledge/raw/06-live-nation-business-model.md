# How Live Nation's Monopoly Works

- **Source:** The American Prospect
- **URL:** https://prospect.org/power/2024-05-24-how-live-nations-monopoly-works/
- **Author:** Luke Goldstein
- **Date:** May 24, 2024

## Content

Live Nation Entertainment dominates the live entertainment industry through a vertically integrated business model that spans every segment of the concert ecosystem. The company operates across venue ownership, ticketing through Ticketmaster, artist management, and event promotion, allowing it to capture revenue at every stage of the concert experience.

In 2024, Live Nation saw 151 million fans attend nearly 55,000 events, with total revenue reaching $23.16 billion, a 3% year-over-year increase. The Concerts segment alone generated $19.02 billion, making up 82% of overall revenue. Ticketmaster processed 637 million tickets during the year. Adjusted operating income reached $2.15 billion, up 14% year over year, and earnings per share more than doubled from $1.34 in 2023 to $2.74 in 2024.

The company's competitive advantage stems from its "flywheel" business model: it promotes shows of company-managed artists at venues where Live Nation is the owner or operator, using Ticketmaster for ticketing. The ticket fees and first-party customer data fund more artists and concerts. Each spin of the flywheel generates momentum, creating a self-reinforcing loop that locks in artists, venues, and fans while keeping competitors out. Ticketmaster holds an estimated 70% or greater market share for major concerts in North America.

Live Nation's digital-first approach processed over 80% of total ticket volume digitally in 2024. With over 200 million unique users, this direct channel enables dynamic pricing, premium offerings, and invaluable first-party data capture. The company operates more than 200 owned venues and has plans to add 20 large venues through 2026, expected to attract 6 to 7 million incremental fans.

A jury found Live Nation and Ticketmaster liable for operating as a monopoly in the ticketing market in April 2026, validating longstanding complaints about competition stifling and fee inflation. Despite regulatory scrutiny, CEO Michael Rapino declared that "2024 was live music's biggest year yet" and projected that 2025 would be even bigger, with stadium show pipeline up 60% and 65 million tickets already sold for 2025 concerts.
